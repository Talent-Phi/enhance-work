Under Florida law, aesthetic injectables, including Botox, Dysport, and dermal fillers, may only be administered by:</p>\n\n<ul>\n<li>Nurse Practitioners (NPs and APRNs)</li>\n<li>Physician Assistants (PAs)</li>\n<li>Physicians (MDs and DOs)</li>\n</ul>\n\n<p>Registered nurses cannot perform injectables in Florida. This is not a supervision question or a collaborative agreement question. There is no protocol structure, no physician oversight model, and no legal workaround that allows an RN to administer injectables in this state. The <a href=\"https://floridasnursing.gov/\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\" style=\"color:#2bbcb0;\">Florida Board of Nursing</a> has been clear on this point, and clinics that allow it are operating outside their legal scope.</p>\n\n<p>Many RNs arrive having heard otherwise, or believing that \"working under a physician\" changes the equation. It does not. If you are an RN in Florida and your goal is to inject, there is one path: become a Nurse Practitioner.</p>\n\n<h2>If You Are an RN Who Wants to Work in Aesthetics</h2>\n\n<p>Not being able to inject does not mean there is no place for you at a med spa. Not a certification course. Not a training weekend. How many patients have you treated yourself, independently, for a specific procedure? That is the number practices care about.</p>\n\n<p>Practices will train on skin treatments. They will train on device protocols. Nobody is going to train an injector from scratch while also paying full injector rates. The cost of building an injector from zero is significant, and practices absorb that cost by starting inexperienced providers at lower base compensation during a training period. That is a reasonable trade for both sides.</p>\n\n<h3>Dermatology background is a real differentiator</h3>\n\n<p>Among clinical backgrounds, dermatology is the one that actually translates to aesthetic practice. Emergency medicine, ICU, and most hospital specialties prepare you for acute clinical work, not for aesthetic consultations or the patient relationship in a cosmetic setting. If you have a dermatology background, lead with it. For everyone else, independent injectable patient count is the number that matters most.</p>\n\n<h3>How you handle the consultation is part of the evaluation</h3>\n\n<p>Aesthetic patients are elective patients. They are paying for a result and an experience. Providers who come from high-acuity hospital environments sometimes struggle with the shift to a service-oriented patient relationship. Practices that hire thoughtfully pay attention to this in the interview, not just to credentials.</p>\n\n<figure style=\"margin:2.5rem 0;text-align:center;\">\n<img src=\"/images/blog/aesthetic-nurse-jobs-interview-1775743792781.webp\" alt=\"Aesthetic nurse practitioner in a med spa job interview in South Florida\" style=\"max-width:100%;border-radius:8px;\" loading=\"lazy\">\n<figcaption style=\"font-size:0.85rem;color:#666;margin-top:0.6rem;\">Most South Florida med spas rely on informal interviews and Indeed postings, which is why placement quality and retention are low across the industry.</figcaption>\n</figure>\n\n<h2>What Aesthetic Nurse Jobs Pay in South Florida</h2>\n\n<p>Here is the version of this conversation that most job postings skip.</p>\n\n<p>For NP injectors in South Florida, base hourly rates typically fall between $50 and $65. On top of that, most practices offer a sales bonus or commission structure tied to revenue generated. An experienced injector with strong conversion and a loyal patient base can earn well above that base through commissions alone.</p>\n\n<p>For new NPs entering aesthetics with no injectable experience, expect compensation closer to the lower end of that range. That is not a slight. It reflects the economics accurately.</p>\n\n<p>When a practice hires someone without aesthetic experience, they are not just paying a salary. They are absorbing the cost of training, the risk of lower patient satisfaction during the learning curve, and the production gap while the provider builds speed and confidence. If you are being hired without a portfolio of injectable patients, you are receiving something that would otherwise cost $15,000 to $25,000 in private training fees. Understanding the first job as an investment, not just a paycheck, is what separates candidates who break into aesthetics quickly from those who spend years waiting for an opportunity that never comes.</p>\n\n<p>The nurses who stall out almost always share the same pattern: hospital-trained, no aesthetic experience, expecting rates tied to a clinical skill set that does not yet exist in this setting. The ones who build careers here accept the equation early and grow through it fast.</p>\n\n<h2>How Hiring Actually Works at South Florida Med Spas</h2>\n\n<p>The honest answer: it is not a structured process at most practices.</p>\n\n<p>Most aesthetic practice owners, whether they are MDs, PAs, or NPs, are clinicians first.
